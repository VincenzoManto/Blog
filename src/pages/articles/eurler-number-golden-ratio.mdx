# Symbolic synthesis of Euler's Number and the Golden Ratio

## Abstract

This paper analyzes the structural configurations generated by symbolic regression driven by genetic algorithms (GAs) when tasked with approximating or defining fundamental mathematical constants ($e$ and $\Phi$). Unbound by human design heuristics—such as the classical preference for visual symmetry and algebraic parsimony—the evolutionary process yields unconventional functional architectures. We examine the mechanics of asymptotic convergence, the automated leveraging of removable singularities, and the algebraic invariance of the evolved continued fractions.

---

## 1. Asymptotic Optimization and Error Damping for Euler's Number ($e$)

During the initial computational phase, the genetic algorithm was constrained to a search space defined by a rational function raised to a linear power. The fitness function prioritized the minimization of the local residual error at a fixed discrete evaluation point ($n = 50$).

The functional output generated at iteration 1499 is expressed as:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} f(n) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left( 1 + \frac{2n - 4}{-8n^2 + 19n - 6} \right)^{-4n + 2}$$

### 1.1 Algebraic Factorization and Singularity Elimination

An inspection of the rational core reveals that the algorithm introduced an intentional, removable singularity at $n = 2$. Factorization of the numerator $P(n)$ and the denominator $Q(n)$ yields:

* $P(n) = 2(n - 2)$
* $Q(n) = -(n - 2)(8n - 3)$

For all evaluation points where $n \neq 2$, the rational expression simplifies via the cancellation of the common linear factor:

$$1 + \frac{2(n - 2)}{-(n - 2)(8n - 3)} = 1 - \frac{2}{8n - 3} = \frac{8n - 5}{8n - 3}$$

### 1.2 Scale Transformation and Convergence Rate Enhancement

Inverting the base argument by changing the sign of the exponent yields the structurally cleaner form:

$$f(n) = \left( \frac{8n - 3}{8n - 5} \right)^{4n - 2}$$

To map the asymptotic trajectory, we introduce a change of variable, setting $k = 4n - 2$, which implies $8n = 2k + 4$. Substituting these terms into the argument normalizes the expression into:

$$f(k) = \left( \frac{2k + 1}{2k - 1} \right)^k$$

This formulation matches d'Alembert's classical symmetric approximation for the limit $\lim_{k \to \infty} f(k) = e$.

From an analytical perspective, the high local precision achieved at $n = 50$ (absolute error $\approx 5.78 \times 10^{-6}$) does not stem from an alteration of the function's fundamental thermodynamic limit. Instead, the GA engineered a **scale compression**. By configuring the genetic coefficients to map the computational constraint $n = 50$ onto an effective index of $k = 198$, the algorithm accelerated convergence along the implicit Taylor series, effectively damping the first-order error to $O(1/k^2)$.

## 2. Algebraic Invariance of the Golden Ratio ($\Phi$) via Generalized Continued Fractions

In the second experiment, the algorithm operated within the geometric domain of generalized continued fractions, optimizing for convergence toward the irrational constant $\Phi \approx 1.6180339887$.

The optimized genetic vectors converged on a periodic, recursive structure parameterized by the coefficients `a=1, b=7, c=7, d=7`:

$$x = 1 + \cfrac{7}{7 + \cfrac{7}{1 + \cfrac{7}{7 + \cfrac{7}{1 + \dots}}}}$$

### 2.1 Verification of Fixed-Point Convergence

To analytically determine the accumulation point of this evolved sequence, we exploit the self-similarity of the infinite continued fraction. The recursive architecture compresses into a single functional equation of a fixed point:

$$x = 1 + \frac{7}{7 + \frac{7}{x}}$$

Solving the relation algebraically:

1. **Normalize the internal denominator:** $7 + \frac{7}{x} = \frac{7x + 7}{x}$
2. **Substitute and invert the primary fraction:** $x = 1 + \frac{7x}{7x + 7}$
3. **Factor and cancel the arbitrary scalar ($7$):** $x = 1 + \frac{7x}{7(x + 1)} \implies x = 1 + \frac{x}{x + 1}$
4. **Multiply by the lowest common denominator $(x + 1)$:** $x(x + 1) = x + 1 + x \implies x^2 + x = 2x + 1$

The resulting quadratic governing equation simplifies to:

$$x^2 - x - 1 = 0$$

The positive real root of this polynomial yields the exact analytical definition of the target constant:

$$x = \frac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2} = \Phi$$

### 2.2 Structural Redundancy and Non-Human Heuristics

The selection of the coefficient $7$ underscores a departure from human mathematical habits. While an operator favors the selection of the identity element—leading to the canonical continued fraction of Fibonacci where all coefficients equal $1$—the genetic algorithm navigated into an infinite algebraic equivalence class.

Through equivalence transformations of continued fractions, any triplet of identical coefficients positioned at these specific homographic nodes undergoes scaling cancellation. The machine selected the scalar $7$ not through top-down deduction, but as a stable trajectory within a random walk validated by automated selection. This confirms that the algebraic stability of the quadratic invariant remains entirely independent of coefficient parsimony.

---

Applying genetic algorithms to symbolic regression highlights the divergence between deductive formal elegance and inductive heuristic efficiency. The solutions analyzed demonstrate that evolutionary processes do not generate novel underlying theorems; instead, they exploit alternate, redundant paths within a solution space. By optimizing the internal geometry of a formula purely for numerical convergence rather than human readability, these systems serve as highly effective tools for uncovering non-trivial mathematical identities.
